Digging into Literature: How to Read
Peer-Reviewed Literature with a Critical Eye

    Unfortunately, articles are regularly published that build on racist frameworks or actively promote the codification of genetic differences between races. We are taught that peer-reviewed literature is the gold standard of science, which can sometimes predispose us to trust the conclusions put forth in peer-reviewed articles, even when the conclusions are based on faulty scientific principles and practices. Here are some questions you can ask to evaluate the legitimacy of articles that reference race.

    In discussing the points here, we will use examples from a problematic article published by Gower and Fowler in the Journal of Internal Medicine, and a Letter to the Editor response by Tsai et al. All cited worked can be found at the bottom of the page.

Questions to Critically Examine Race in Scientific Literature
1. How do the authors define race?
2. Do the authors connect race and genetic difference or race and physiology?
3. Do the authors include a discussion of social determinants of health?
4. Is the logic circular?
5. Do the authors use outdated or stigmatized terminology?
6. Do the authors reference racism or only race?
7. What to do next?

How do the authors define race?

“Although black people have lower rates of Stage 3 chronic kidney disease (CKD) compared to white people, they have higher rates of Stage 4 CKD and end-stage renal disease (NIDDK 2016, Delanaye & Mariat, 2013). So, although black people are less likely to be diagnosed with earlier stages of kidney disease, they disproportionately feel the burden of its later manifestations.”
Trnka (2019)

Estimated glomerular filtration rate is an assessment of an individual’s baseline kidney function based on variables such as serum creatinine, sex, age, and race. Race correction involves the addition of a multiplier to the eGFR equation if the individual is identified as Black. The use of race in the calculation of eGFR implies a biological difference between Black individuals and individuals of other races. The perpetuation of this misconception leads to bias in the field of medicine and contributes to disparities in care for Black patients.

Key Points

  • Tracing the "citation geneology" of the eGFR race correction brings to light how historical use of the correction has re-capitulated its value between generations of providers, with no clear examination of patient benefit or harm.
  • There is no scientifically established rationale published within the peer-reviewed literature for using race in the calculation of eGFR.

About

Our goal is to help organize and distill the ever-growing body of work on racism in medicine. We hope this project will be a resource to those advocating for antiracism in healthcare - from patients and providers to students, administrators, and policy makers who are pushing for change.

If you have feedback or want to contribute content, please reach out! This is a work in progress. We’re always looking for new resources, perspectives, and thoughts on what makes people listen.
