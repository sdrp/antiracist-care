Topic 2

Hyperbilirubinemia

Authors: Angela Y. Zhang (she/hers), MD, University of Washington
Reviewers: Name, pronouns, degree, institution/location

Topic & Clinical Relevance

  • Hyperbilirubinemia refers to the buildup of bilirubin in the bloodstream. Bilirubin is a normal byproduct when our red blood cells break down at the end of their life cycle, and is passed through our liver and gut into feces. In babies, this system is immature and thus all babies have relatively higher bilirubin, which can show up as yellowed skin, or jaundice. For various reasons, the bilirubin may become higher than expected, and at very high levels can lead to brain damage.
  • For this reason, it is routine to measure bilirubin in the newborn period, starting as early as at 24 hours of life.
  • What clinical action is appropriate for a given level of bilirubin is determined by a few calculators - historically BiliTool, and more recently NCNC. For a given bilirubin on BiliTool, East Asian race is cited as a risk factor that might prompt a provider to consider differential follow-up. Of note, other risk factors are biologically reasonable, such as blood type incompatibility.

Frequent Misconceptions

  • Isn’t there a genetic association between East Asian race and hyperbilirubinemia?
    • UpToDate's article "Unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia in the newborn: Pathogenesis and etiology" cites 2 papers for its claim that there is a common mutation in the UGT1A1 gene in Eastern Asian infants, which leads to an increased incidence of severe neonatal hyperbilirubinemia. However, these two papers consist of study populations with small N’s, as well as specific populations not generalizable to socially defined race.
      • Reference 12: A case-control study in Shenzen, China with n < 300 for both cases and controls, followed by a systematic review of the literature with most studies also having n < 200. Of note, the “Caucasian” population consists of only Turkish and American studies. (NB: Caucasian is no longer the preferred term given the eugenecist roots of the term)
      • Reference 13: 42 Japanese newborns and 50 healthy German controls
  • Doesn’t jaundice resolve at different time points in infants of different races?
    • UpToDate cites a paper from Pediatrics to assert that visible jaundice resolves in different time frames in white, East Asian, and African-American infants.
      • Reference 9: Not only did the Pediatrics article in Reference 9 have a markedly skewed racial representation (figure), the paper states: “Our objective was to document the natural history of jaundice in the first month in a predominantly breastfed, white population of newborns, to identify the prevalence of prolonged hyperbilirubinemia and the clinical observation of jaundice in these infants, and to evaluate the utility of the cephalocaudal zone score as a screen for hyperbilirubinemia in office-based pediatric practice.” In other words, the paper’s aim was never to document racial differences and thus extrapolating as such even with significant results feels like an overreach.

How Adjustment Contributes to Health Inequity

  • Hyper-vigilance in East Asian infants for considering hyperbilirubinemia, thus possibly focusing care away from other groups

Possible Solutions

  • Remove East Asian as a risk factor - in fact, the newer NCNC calculator does not mention race at all and hopefully is being more widely adopted.
  • Universal screening of bilirubin with a transcutaneous meter at 24 hours of life

Takeaway Points

  • While predisposition to hyperbilirubinemia may be genetically clustered, current data poorly define race, use small sample sizes, and extrapolate sub-ethnic groups to an entire socially defined category.
  • Health equity is important to consider, only not in the form of this risk factor. Jaundice is harder to assess in darker skin, partially due to the lack of representation in medical school curriculum.
